# On the Taṣṭinupuku Pinnaganyāḍi
The folder named `Taṣṭinupuku Pinnaganyāḍi` is, essentially, the ultimate lexical encyclopaedia for this world. It, ideally, will contain all information on all languages (from a Misadinian bias pov), however it will largely focus on Sōmyamu Mīsāḍinu (SM) (Classical Misadinu).

To get a general jist of the time period of SM, it was spoken in a time of around the Mauryans or the initial rise of Rome (the century before the end of the Republic). Before it came Pāta Mīsāḍinu (PM) (Old Misadinu), and before that Prānta Mīsāḍinu (PrM) (Ancient Misadinu).

From PrM, which may also be called `Proto-Anc-2` in some notes or files, came not only PM but also another language known as West Prakāri (WP), which is also the exonym for the people who speak it. For completion, the WP call the Mīsāḍinians the *East Prakāri*.

### Navigation of the TP
Under any language you'll find these main folders: `/Nouns`, `/Adjectives`, `Interrogatives` (question words), and `Verbs`. You might also find `/Pronouns` and `/Adverbs`. 

Further, there'll be table documents that outline various grammatical in or suffixes such as [[S. Mīsāḍinu - Postpositions]], [[Verb Conjugations]], and [[Declensions]].

### About Sōmyamu Mīsāḍinu
SM is an agglutinative language, loosely based on the South Indian Dravidian language of Telugu. This means that:
- Words are formed by stringing together other morphemes (parts of words), instead of other types of languages wherein word forms change slightly to reflect meaning (agglutinative def). Wikipedia (with examples): https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agglutinative_language

- Words *always* end in a vowel. SM has two types of vowels -- dark (`o, i, ō,ī`) and light (`a, u, ā, ū`), with `e, ē` acting as 'transition' vowels (that is, its both light and dark, but leans more towards dark). Adjacent consonant clusters may only have one type of vowel, and so-called 'light consonant clusters' cannot be adjacent to 'dark consonant clusters' without an intermediate 'transition consonant cluster' (i.e. one with `e, ē`) in between. For example:

	 [[sāgevōni]] (field or topic): The `sā` cluster is a light cluster, and the `vō` and `ni` clusters are both dark. In order to transition from light to dark, the intermediate `ge` transition cluster is required.

SM nouns work as follows:
- Words use declension, see [[Declensions]] (the file contains case definitions).
- For any term where no relevant declension exists, the *oblique* case is applied to the word, and then a post position is used: [[S. Mīsāḍinu - Postpositions]].

SM verbs work as follows:
- See [[Verb Conjugations]].
- Verbs have three parts: The stem (with the base word meaning), the aspect-tense (the middle part), and the person (the last part). For example:
		*divyakōcasācalpu*
		*cal* -- The verb stem from [[Divyākina]], meaning 'to buy'.
		*kōca* -- The continuous-future aspect-tense (i.e. 'will be buying').
		*sācalpu* -- Neutral third person plural
		Thus, the full meaning is *they (neutr.) will be buying*.

- Adverbs are indicated by putting the `-nā` suffix after a noun or adjective.
- Participles work in a very similar suffix-oriented way to conjugations, just see [[Verb Conjugations]].

SM Word order:
- SM has a very flexible word order, but the official and literary one is:
Subject, object, verb.

- Adjectives officially come after the noun they describe, and must have adjectival agreement. 

- The possessor always comes *before* the possessee.
- Question words TYPICALLY go *just* before the verb.
