# A Guide to Sōmyamu Mīsāḍinu

*Sōmyamu Mīsāḍinu* (SM), or **Classical Mīsāḍinu**, is the prestige literary register of the Mīsāḍinu language family. It was spoken and written roughly in the era corresponding to the Mauryan empire or the late Roman Republic. It descended from [[Pāta Mīsāḍinu]] (Old Mīsāḍinu), which itself descended from [[Prānta Mīsāḍinu]] (Ancient Mīsāḍinu, also called *Proto-Anc-2*). The same ancestor also gave rise to *West Prakāri* — the language of the people to the west, who in turn called the Mīsāḍinians the *East Prakāri*.

SM is an **agglutinative** language, meaning that words are formed by chaining morphemes together rather than by internal vowel changes or fusing word forms. It is loosely modelled on the South Indian Dravidian language Telugu, sharing its phonological character and much of its grammatical logic.

---

## Phonology

### Consonant Inventory

SM's consonant inventory is derived directly from Telugu — with one major simplification: **aspirated consonants are absent**. Telugu distinguishes voiced from voiceless stops and each has both an aspirated and unaspirated form (*p/ph, b/bh, k/kh, g/gh*, etc.); SM has collapsed all aspirated forms onto their unaspirated equivalents.

The consonants, using IAST transliteration:

| Manner | Labial | Dental | Retroflex | Palatal | Velar | Glottal |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Stop (voiceless) | p | t | ṭ | c | k | — |
| Stop (voiced) | b | d | ḍ | — | g | — |
| Nasal | m | n | ṇ | — | — | — |
| Fricative | v | s | ṣ | — | — | h |
| Lateral | — | l | ḷ | — | — | — |
| Rhotic | — | r | — | — | — | — |
| Approximant | — | — | — | y | — | — |

**Retroflex consonants** (ṭ, ḍ, ṇ, ṣ, ḷ) are pronounced with the tongue curled back to touch the roof of the mouth — a sound characteristic of Dravidian and South Asian languages generally.

### Vowels

SM has ten vowel phonemes — five short and five long:

| Short | Long | Quality |
| --- | --- | --- |
| a | ā | Low central / open |
| i | ī | High front |
| u | ū | High back |
| e | ē | Mid front |
| o | ō | Mid-high back / round |

Long vowels are held approximately twice as long as short ones. All vowels can appear at the start, middle, or end of a word.

**Words in SM always end in a vowel.** There are no word-final consonants.

### IAST Transliteration

SM is romanised using the International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration (IAST). Key characters:

| Character | Pronunciation note |
| --- | --- |
| ā, ī, ū, ē, ō | Long vowels — held longer than a, i, u, e, o |
| ṭ | Retroflex t — tongue curled back |
| ḍ | Retroflex d |
| ṇ | Retroflex n |
| ṣ | Retroflex sibilant — like *sh* but retroflex |
| ḷ | Retroflex lateral — like *l* but retroflex |

## Vowel Harmony

SM has a **vowel harmony** system: vowels are divided into *light* and *dark* classes, and within any sequence of adjacent syllables only one type may appear. The two classes are:

- **Light vowels:** a, u, ā, ū
- **Dark vowels:** o, i, ō, ī
- **Transition vowels:** e, ē — neutral, usable on either side, but lean dark

Adjacent syllable clusters must share a vowel class. A *light* cluster cannot sit directly next to a *dark* cluster — a **transition cluster** (containing e or ē) must be placed between them.

**Example:** [[sāgevōni]] (*field, topic*)

> *sā* — light cluster (long a)
> *ge* — transition cluster (e)
> *vō* — dark cluster (long o)
> *ni* — dark cluster (i)

The *sā* cluster is light; *vō* and *ni* are dark. The transition syllable *ge* bridges them. Without it, the word would violate harmony.

This harmony rule shapes how suffixes attach to words: a suffix with dark vowels cannot directly follow a light-vowelled stem without a transitional element, and vice versa.

## Nouns

### Declension Overview

SM nouns are inflected (declined) to show their role in the sentence. There are **three declension types**, determined by the final vowel of the nominative singular:

- **B-Type** — nouns ending in *-u* (the most common class)
- **C-Type** — nouns ending in *-i*
- **A-Type** — used for pronouns

The nine cases and their rough meanings:

| Case | Meaning / Use |
| --- | --- |
| **Nominative** | Subject of the sentence |
| **Accusative** | Direct object |
| **Genitive / Oblique** | Possession; also the base for postpositional phrases |
| **Ablative** | Movement away from (*from, out of*) |
| **Locative** | Position inside or at (*in, at*) |
| **Instrumental** | Accompaniment or means (*with, by means of*) |
| **Dative** | Indirect object (*to, for*) |
| **Adessive** | Location at/upon/adjacent to (distinct from locative) |

### B-Type Declension

For nouns whose citation form ends in *-u* — e.g. [[Bōjastu]] (*neck*), [[Tīvunu]] (*tower*).

|  | Singular | Plural |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Nom.** | -u | -upu |
| **Acc.** | -a | -upa |
| **Gen./Obl.** | -uku | -upaku |
| **Abl.** | -uju | -upaju |
| **Loc.** | -ulu | -upalu |
| **Instru.** | -uvu | -upavu |
| **Dat.** | -ucāku | -upacuku |
| **Add.** | -uppana | -upappana |

**Example:** *Bōjastu* → Gen. *Bōjastuku* (of the neck); Loc. *Bōjastulu* (at the neck); Dat. *Bōjastucāku* (for the neck).

### C-Type Declension

For nouns whose citation form ends in *-i* — e.g. [[Arada]] (wait — see below; most *-i* nouns follow this).

|  | Singular | Plural |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Nom.** | -i | -īpi |
| **Acc.** | -li | -īpēli |
| **Gen./Obl.** | -iki | -īpēki |
| **Abl.** | -iji | -īpije |
| **Loc.** | -ili | -īpili |
| **Instru.** | -ivi | -īpave |
| **Dat.** | -iceki | -īpecāki |
| **Add.** | -īppēni | -īpappēni |

### Using the Oblique + Postposition

When no dedicated case expresses a needed meaning, the noun is placed in the **oblique** (Gen./Obl.) form and a postposition follows it. This is the general-purpose strategy for any relationship not covered by the eight cases above. See the [[S. Mīsāḍinu - Postpositions|Postpositions]] section below.

### Personal Pronouns

| Person | Singular | Plural |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **1st** | *gunnu* (gunu) | *mīppu* (mī) |
| **2nd** | *kēnnu* (kē) | *kēppu* |
| **3rd neut.** | *calnu* (ca) | *caluppu* (calu) |
| **3rd masc.** | *yābu* (yā) | *caluppu* (calu) |
| **3rd fem.** | *yāsu* (yā) | *caluppu* (calu) |

Third-person plural *caluppu* covers all genders for *thinking* beings (humans, etc.). The *non-thinker* category covers animals, objects, and abstractions — a distinction SM marks consistently.

### Formal and Divine Registers

SM has **register-specific pronouns** for formal and religious contexts:

| | 2nd sg | 2nd pl | 3rd sg |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Formal (thinker)** | [[kvēnnu]] | [[kvēppu]] | [[kvalnu]] |
| **Divine** | [[dōnnu]] | [[dōppu]] | [[dōlnu]] |

The divine register is used when addressing or referring to deities or figures of supreme veneration.

## Adjectives

Adjectives in SM must agree with the noun they modify. They officially appear **after** the noun they describe.

**Agreement:** When an adjective modifies a noun, it takes the same case suffix as the noun. This ensures both elements decline together through a sentence.

**Comparison:**
- Comparative (*-er*): add *-krā* to the adjective
- Superlative (*-est*): add *-ṣa* to the adjective

**Adjectival derivation:** Any noun can be turned into an adjective by adding *-agyāra* (from the past participle of [[āggayākina]]). This gives a meaning roughly like "having the quality of X" or "X-like".

**Examples:** [[āgeccu (āg)]] (*big, large*) → *āgeccukrā* (*bigger*) → *āgeccuṣa* (*biggest*). [[Sāgyu]] (*small*) is the antonym; its prefix form *sāg-* means *sub-* or *minor*.

## Verbs

### Verb Structure

Every conjugated SM verb consists of three chained parts:

```
STEM + TENSE-ASPECT + PERSON ENDING
```

1. **Stem** — the core meaning of the verb. Dictionary (infinitive) forms end in *-kina*; the stem is what remains when *-kina* is removed.
2. **Tense-aspect marker** — encodes both *when* (past / present / future) and *how* (continuous / perfect / stative / simple / perfect-continuous).
3. **Person ending** — matches the subject (which pronoun is doing the action).

**Example** from the *[[Phrases (Taṣṭiyēvayānupu)|Phrases]]* file:

> *Gunnu yānēdi chalpāragunnu*
> ("I have been there")
>
> *chalp-* — stem of *to go*
> *-pāra-* — perfect present (*have [done]*)
> *-gunnu* — first person singular

### Tense-Aspect Table

|  | Past | Present | Future |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Continuous** | -kōva- | -kōra- | -kōca- |
| **Perfect** | -pāva- | -pāra- | -pāca- |
| **Stative** | -tēva- | -tēra- | -tēca- |
| **Simple** | *(none)* | -naira- | -naica- |
| **Perfect Continuous** | -ḷima- | -ḷima- | -ḷica- |

The **continuous** aspect describes ongoing action (*was/is/will be doing*). The **perfect** aspect describes a completed action with present relevance (*have/has done*). The **stative** aspect describes a state resulting from an action (*is in the state of having done*). The **simple** aspect has no past form (the language uses the perfect past for completed past events). The **perfect continuous** describes an action that has been ongoing and continues (*has been doing*).

### Person Endings

The person ending mirrors the nominative form of the personal pronoun. The subject pronoun can often be dropped from the sentence when the verb form is unambiguous.

**Example conjugation** of *Calyākina* (*to go / to try*), present continuous:

| Person | Form | Gloss |
| --- | --- | --- |
| I | *cal-kōra-gunnu* | I am going |
| We | *cal-kōra-mīppu* | We are going |
| You (sg) | *cal-kōra-kēnnu* | You are going |
| They (neut) | *cal-kōra-caluppu* | They are going |
| He | *cal-kōra-yābu* | He is going |
| She | *cal-kōra-yāsu* | She is going |

### Moods

Beyond the indicative (statement of fact), SM marks several moods:

| Mood | Suffix | Use |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Subjunctive** | *-sōḍi* | Hypothetical, wishes, doubt |
| **Conditional** | *-ṭeṅka* | If-clauses |
| **Imperative (present)** | *-vakka* | Commands for immediate action |
| **Imperative (future)** | *-cakka* | Commands for later action |
| **Potential** | *-pōḷu-* | Ability or possibility (*can, could*) |
| **Permissive** | *-kala-* | Permission (*may, is allowed to*) — from [[kvalnu]] |
| **Causative** | *-vā-* | Making someone else do the action (*cause to*) |

The **potential** and **permissive** moods are inflective — they take a position in the stem-aspect-person chain like tense markers do, rather than standing alone at the end.

## Participles

Participles allow a verb to function as a modifier (like an English *-ing* or *-ed* form). SM has positive and negative forms across three tenses:

|  | Positive | Negative |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Past** | *-yāra* | *-sāyāra* |
| **Present** | *-yāva* | *-sāyāva* |
| **Future** | *-yāca* | *-sāyāca* |

**Using participles with [[yōganci]]:** A participle followed by *yōganci* creates a *while/when* clause:

| Phrase | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| *Kōḍayāva yōganci* | While going |
| *Kōḍayāra yōganci* | When (someone) went |
| *Kōḍasāyāca yōganci* | While (someone) will not be going |

## Postpositions

Where English uses prepositions (*on, under, through, about*) placed before a noun, SM places equivalent words **after** the noun, in the oblique case. These are called postpositions.

Core postpositions:

| Postposition | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| [[Keṇṭi]] | Behind |
| [[Vastu]] | Under, down |
| [[Irānō]] | Through; by (of a maker or author) |
| [[Sāccaka]] | Above, up |
| [[pannu]] | Near |
| [[Ta]] | Hearsay marker (*apparently, reportedly*) |
| [[poni]] | From; origin of |
| [[pōje]] | Before (in time) |
| [[dailu]] | About, concerning |
| [[nākya]] | Like (*verb form — to be like*) |
| [[nākku]] | Like (*noun form*) |
| [[Yānu]] | Verb → noun nominaliser |
| *-tanam* | *-ness* (abstract quality) |
| *-koba* | For, benefactive (*for the benefit of*) |
| *-gilde(mi)* | Than, compared to |
| *-krā* | Comparative (*-er*) |
| *-ṣa* | Superlative (*-est*) |

**Derivational postpositions** — postpositions that change the grammatical category of a word:

| Postposition | Function |
| --- | --- |
| [[nā]] | Adverb marker — turns a noun or adjective into an adverb |
| *-agyāra* | Noun → adjective (*having the quality of*) |
| *-nu* | Indicates a human referent |
| [[Yānu]] | Verb → noun (*the act of [verb]-ing*) |

## Word Formation and Derivation

SM's agglutinative nature means new words are built by stacking morphemes. A few productive patterns:

**Noun → Adjective:** Attach *-agyāra* to any noun to make an adjective meaning roughly "of/like [noun]".

**Adjective / Noun → Adverb:** Attach the postposition [[nā]] to make an adverb. *e.g.* a word for *strong* + *-nā* = *strongly*.

**Verb → Noun:** Attach [[Yānu]] (the nominaliser) to the verbal stem to make the action a noun — the equivalent of English *-tion*, *-ing*, or *-ment*.

**Abstract quality:** *-tanam* attached to any noun or adjective produces an abstract noun (like English *-ness* or *-ity*).

**Benefactive:** *-koba* marks *for whom* something is done — equivalent to English *for* in *I did it for you*.

**Prefix *sāg-*:** The short form of [[Sāgyu]] (*small*) functioning as a prefix meaning *sub-* or *minor-*. For example, a *sub-section* would be a *sāg-* compound of the word for section.

---

## Word Order

SM word order is **flexible** — because case suffixes mark grammatical role, the order of constituents does not change the basic meaning. The **canonical literary order** is:

> **Subject — Object — Verb**

Adjectives **officially come after** the noun they modify, and must carry adjectival agreement (matching case).

**Possessives:** The possessor always precedes the possessee — *the king's daughter* would be ordered *king's-GEN daughter*.

**Question words:** Interrogatives (*who, what, where*, etc.) typically appear **immediately before the verb**.

## Sample Sentences

**Universal Declaration (Article 1):**

> *Pinna calnupu unṭaiḍyupalū kōkyupalu yēṣoyārdyanā gunḍe cālakēḍyātēvachaluppu.*
>
> "All human beings are born free and equal in dignity and rights. They are endowed with reason and conscience and should act towards one another in a spirit of brotherhood."

**Growth through adversity:**

> *[[Ankara|Gu Ankara ankarakirānō]] [[Kostagayākina|kostaganaicamīppu]].*
>
> "Through adversity, we grow."

**A vow:**

> *Kanconikipōje sāggakēyānu.*
>
> "Life before death. Strength before weakness. Journey before destination."
